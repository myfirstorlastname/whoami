"Getting in is only half the job—true impact comes after persistence, movement, and data exfiltration."

🧠 About

This page documents my completion of the Post-Compromise module, part of the Red Teaming learning path on TryHackMe. This stage covers what happens after initial access has been achieved: maintaining presence, escalating privileges, moving laterally, and ultimately achieving mission objectives such as data theft.

The emphasis is on methodology and tradecraft: working quietly, chaining techniques, and persisting beyond initial footholds. Unlike exploitation, post-compromise requires patience, creativity, and discipline to avoid detection.

🛰️ Philosophy

"Exploitation is loud, but persistence is art."
  • Every foothold is temporary—persistence is mandatory.
  • Enumeration is not just recon: it’s a roadmap for escalation and movement.
  • Post-compromise success is measured by objectives achieved, not shells obtained.

🔎 Learning Outcomes: Post-Compromise Module

From privilege escalation to exfiltration—turning access into lasting control.

📘 Module Summary (Rooms)

  • The Lay of the Land — Understanding the compromised environment, identifying trust boundaries, and mapping critical assets.
  • Enumeration — Collecting host and domain information, credentials, tokens, and identifying privilege escalation paths.
  • Windows Privilege Escalation — Techniques such as unquoted service paths, DLL hijacking, misconfigured permissions, and kernel exploits.
  • Windows Local Persistence — Methods like scheduled tasks, registry keys, WMI event subscriptions, and service creation to maintain access.
  • Lateral Movement and Pivoting — Leveraging harvested credentials or tokens to access other systems, moving across network segments, and pivoting through compromised hosts.
  • Data Exfiltration — Covert techniques for extracting sensitive data while evading monitoring (encryption, staging, chunking, living-off-the-land tools).

🧪 What I Appreciated

  • Clear methodology: from foothold → escalation → persistence → lateral movement → exfiltration.
  • Realistic Windows-based scenarios that mirror enterprise environments.
  • Hands-on practice with persistence techniques that defenders actively look for.
  • Emphasis on operational security (OPSEC) and stealth during enumeration and movement.

🎓 Who I Recommend This To

  • Red Team beginners who want to understand what happens after “getting in”.
  • Pentesters preparing for advanced exams (e.g., OSCP, OSEP) that require escalation and persistence skills.
  • Defenders (Blue Team) who need to recognize post-compromise behaviors in SIEM/EDR alerts.

🛠️ My Personal Tips

  • Always start with enumeration: know your environment before attempting escalation.
  • Privilege escalation is rarely “one shot”—test multiple vectors quietly.
  • For persistence, use minimal and reversible methods (low footprint) when possible.
  • Lateral movement should mimic normal admin activity to avoid detection.
  • Stage exfiltration: compress, encrypt, and move data in chunks.
