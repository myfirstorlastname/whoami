Communication in SOC Level 1

Good hacks are invisible. Good reports make them unforgettable.

1. Why Communication Is Crucial in a SOC

In a Security Operations Center (SOC), communication is a fundamental pillar to ensure operational continuity, proper incident handling, and effective collaboration between analysts.

A Level 1 (L1) analyst, though junior in the technical hierarchy, is often the first responder to an alert. They must:

  • Quickly understand the context of an alert
  • Document it clearly and actionably
  • Efficiently relay it to the team, the next shift, or upper levels

2. Day-to-Day Operational Communication

Here are the main forms of communication expected daily in a SOC:

Tickets & ITSM Tools

  • Common tools: ServiceNow, Jira, GLPI, TheHive, etc.
  • Best practices:
    • Clear title: [SIEM] Possible Brute-force on OWA
    • Timestamp all key events (UTC + local)
    • Concise yet complete description: source, detection method, alert type
    • Attach evidence: logs, screenshots, alert ID
    • Document actions taken: lookups, whois, enrichment
    • Propose severity level and escalation if needed

Shift Handover & Internal Communication

  • Daily briefings or handover meetings: current incidents, escalations
  • Document ongoing alerts with clear status: under analysis, waiting for N2 response, etc.
  • Use instant channels like Slack, Mattermost, or MS Teams

Escalation to L2/L3

When an incident is beyond L1's scope, it must be escalated clearly. A good escalation includes:

  • Summary: “SSH brute-force attempt from persistent suspicious IP despite blocking.”
  • Evidence: logs, screenshots, SIEM details, alert ID
  • Context: frequency, repetition, potential impact
  • Expectations: confirmation, action from L2/L3, firewall block, etc.

3. Professional Written Communication

  • Use a simple, clear, neutral tone (avoid judgment)
  • Favor short sentences: subject + verb + object
  • Structure visually: bullet points, paragraphs, spacing
  • Use standard acronyms (IOC, IP, SIEM, MITRE, etc.)

4. Verbal Communication

As an L1 analyst, you will also be involved in verbal interactions such as:

  • Team briefings (stand-up meetings)
  • Incident debriefs or post-mortems
  • Occasionally, presenting findings to a security manager

Your tone should be calm, precise, and factual. You should be able to explain a technical alert in simple, accurate terms.

5. Example: L1 Incident Ticket


[Title] : [SIEM] SSH Brute-force on Bastion - Russian IP

[Timestamp] : 2025-07-15 09:42 UTC

[Source] : Alert triggered by Splunk (use-case: bruteforce_ssh)

[Description] :
Over 80 SSH login attempts within 10 minutes on the admin bastion (internal IP: 10.10.0.1).
Source IP: 185.202.X.X (ASN OVH - RU)

[Evidence] : Splunk logs, correlation screenshot, VirusTotal lookup (suspicious)

[Actions Taken by L1] :
- Identified IP as malicious
- No successful login
- No other alerts on that host

[Escalation] : Suggested temporary firewall block

Conclusion

Being a good SOC Level 1 analyst goes beyond understanding alerts. It's about relaying them clearly, without distortion, to teammates or higher levels. Strong communication helps anticipate risks, avoid mistakes, and streamline incident response workflows.

Clear, concise, and contextual : your ability to communicate findings determines their real-world value.
