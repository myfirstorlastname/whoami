üïµ Professional activity: Bug Bounty Hunter

üí• HackerOne

  • While upgrading my skills, I still had no professional cybersecurity role. I thus became a bug bounty hunter on HackerOne.
    • I had no prior real-world experience in webhacking.
    • I gathered intel on: what is a scope, a bug bounty program, a report.
    • I challenged my first bug, and this mission lasted one month. It was a medium-sized website with basic functionalities, and the program was open to all.
    • I chose this program because it was recently opened and was likely to have some vulnerabilities.
    • However, recent programs never reward hunters even if the hunter submit a report containing an exploitable critical vulnerability on a key asset.
    • This initial mission allowed me to apply my technical skills in a real-world context, resulting in the submission of several valid reports.
    • After this I tried old programs with rewards, but I found in one year of work less than 5 valid exploitables vulns on such programs.
    • The reason being that these older programs are already 99% secure in this process:
      • The entreprise validates the security of their website through a private team of pentesters.
      • They open a bug bounty program to the 10 best bug bounty hunters, that will clear any remaining vulnerability.
      • Eventually, a public program is created where I, and all bug bounty hunters in the world can search vulnerabilities.
      • One may wonder why such programs are made public if they are already considered secure.
      • The rationale is that websites change with every update, and new zero-day vulnerabilities emerge frequently. This is the company's view of the bug bounty hunter's role.
      • As a result, only the top-ranked hunters typically earn a sustainable income through this activity.
    • Ultimately, all of the valid reports I submitted on older programs were marked as duplicates.
    • Duplicate reports are generally not rewarded, as recognition is typically granted on a first-come, first-served basis.
  • Despite all the above downsides of this activity, I learnt more in this year than I could in professional roles:
    • How to approach the recon phase.
    • How to gather specific intel from resources on the internet: resembling open-source intelligence (OSINT) methodologies
    • Where to expect vulnerabilities: Profile page, custom error page
    • How to exploit the vulnerability, and to quickly assess whether an exploit path is viable or not.
    • How to chain attacks to further exploit different minor vulnerabilities: IDOR+XSS
    • How to write a custom report: Good practices, Reproducible exploit (screenshots), Mitigations and Recommendations
